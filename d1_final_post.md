# Making Public EV Charging Geographically Fair Across Greater Sydney to Advance SDG 11.2: Sustainable Cities and Communities

Public electric-vehicle (EV) charging is becoming essential infrastructure for New South Wales' low-carbon transport transition. SDG 11.2 calls for "access to safe, affordable, accessible and sustainable transport systems for all"; because a public charger is what makes the EV — a clean transport mode — genuinely usable for drivers who cannot charge at home, fair access to charging sits squarely inside this target. EV uptake in NSW is real and accelerating: the state held 52,572 registered EVs by January 2024, 7.5 times the 6,160 recorded in 2021, and EVs reached 9.5% of new-car sales (about 28,200 vehicles) in 2024. As demand grows, whether residents can charge determines whether they switch, yet access is not distributed fairly.

Joining Transport for NSW charging data with ABS 2024-25 population, public plugs per 10,000 residents vary roughly 20-fold across the 34 Greater Sydney LGAs: affluent Woollahra has 16.5 versus a Greater Sydney average of 4.0, while high-population outer suburbs sit far below — Cumberland 0.7, Fairfield 0.9, Canterbury-Bankstown 1.5. These outer-suburban communities have the most apartments and least private off-street parking, so residents are least able to charge at home yet least served. The NSW DCCEEW EV public-charging grant team, which sets the grant funding rules, can directly correct this.

The evidence makes this action low-risk: the score is one measured number from two existing open datasets, so it already ranks all 34 LGAs, pinpoints who is worst-served, and can be re-checked each funding round without new data or subjective judgement.

I therefore call on that team to add a transparent "Charging Access Equity Score" — public plugs per 10,000 residents — to the next co-funding round, and to prioritise the high-population LGAs that fall below the 4.0 average, with a 2030 target of bringing every LGA up to at least 4.0.

They would act because it delivers the largest access gain per dollar, directly advances the NSW EV Strategy and net-zero-by-2050 goals, and is publicly defensible while reusing existing open data at near-zero cost.
