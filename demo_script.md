# Demo Script (~3 minutes)

---

## Part 1 — Dataset Source (~50s)

My project asks: how can NSW planners make the shift to electric vehicles geographically fair — specifically, by improving public EV charging access in outer-suburban areas? This is tied to SDG 11, Sustainable Cities and Communities.

To answer that, I use two government open datasets. The first is from Transport for NSW via Data.NSW — it records every public EV charging site across NSW, including the number of plugs and which Local Government Area it belongs to. The second is from the ABS — Regional Population 2024-25, which gives me the estimated resident population for all 129 NSW LGAs. By combining these two datasets, I can calculate a per-capita metric — public charging plugs per 10,000 residents — which is the fairest way to compare access across areas with very different population sizes.

---

## Part 2 — The Graph (~50s)

So here is my final chart. It's a horizontal bar chart comparing the 10 most under-served and the 5 best-served Greater Sydney LGAs, by public charging plugs per 10,000 residents.

The bars in red are the bottom 10 — all below the Greater Sydney average, shown by this dashed line. The bar in blue at the top is Woollahra, the best-served LGA, included as a reference. And the gap in the middle indicates there are 19 other LGAs I've omitted to keep the chart focused.

The contrast is immediately clear. At the bottom, you see outer and south-western suburbs — Cumberland, Fairfield, Canterbury-Bankstown, Liverpool — large-population areas with very few public chargers. At the top, inner-city suburbs like Woollahra, Waverley, and North Sydney. The gap is dramatic: Woollahra has about 16.5 plugs per 10,000 residents, while Cumberland has only 0.7 — that's over a 20-times difference.

---

## Part 3 — Why This Graph (~1 min)

So why did I design the graph this way?

First, the background. EV adoption is growing fast in NSW, but the charging network isn't keeping up equally everywhere. Residents in outer suburbs often live in higher-density housing without private garages, which means they depend more on public chargers — yet they have the least access. That's the equity problem this project focuses on.

The purpose of this chart is to let a planner look at it and immediately see which LGAs need investment the most. It directly supports my call to action — that planners should prioritise charging rollout in under-served outer suburbs, rather than letting infrastructure concentrate where it already exists.

And the design choices all serve that purpose. I show only the bottom 10 and top 5 instead of all 34 LGAs, because that maximises clarity and contrast without losing integrity — the gap label honestly shows middle LGAs were omitted. I use a per-capita metric instead of raw counts, because raw counts would falsely rank large LGAs as well-served. I focus on Greater Sydney to avoid distortion from tiny regional towns with highway chargers. The red colour draws the eye to the problem areas. The average line provides an objective benchmark. And the takeaway title states the message upfront — this is an argument, not a data dump.

In short, this graph turns two open government datasets into a decision-ready tool for planners.

Thank you.
